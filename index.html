05 · Trade competencies

Units I've already ticked off.

Nationally recognised electrotechnology units I completed in a pre-apprenticeship. That's a head start most applicants don't have on day one. Statement of attainment available on request.

UEECD0007 — Apply WHS regulations, codes and practices safety

What it is: the core electrotechnology safety unit. It covers applying WHS regulations, codes of practice and safe work procedures on an electrotechnology site.

What it means I can do: spot the hazards before I start, apply and check the controls, pick and use the right PPE, and confirm a supply has been isolated by an authorised person before any work begins.

Why it matters: this is what everyone goes home on. I take isolation, tag-out and procedure seriously because I've been trained to do it properly.

UEECD0019 — Fabricate, assemble and dismantle utilities components hands-on

What it is: a hands-on fitting and metal-fabrication unit for making, assembling and pulling apart the kinds of components used across the utilities industry.

What it means I can do: work safely with hand and power tools; measure, mark out and read a drawing; cut, shape, join and thread metal; and dismantle gear methodically so it goes back together right.

Why it matters: it's the practical side of the trade, and the real skill behind the coffee cart I built. Not a weekend hobby.

UEECD0051 — Use drawings, diagrams, standards and specifications schematics

What it is: reading and applying technical documentation: drawings, schematics, schedules, Australian Standards, codes and specs, and the knack of pulling the right detail out for the job.

What it means I can do: find the current drawing for a task, read the symbols and conventions on wiring and circuit diagrams, and apply them accurately.

Why it matters: reading schematics is foundational on the tools. It's the same literacy that lets me follow protection and control diagrams on a grid that's going digital.
