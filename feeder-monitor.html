Distribution feeder monitor.

A hands-on extra-low-voltage build, documented to AS/NZS standards. It senses a simulated feeder (voltage, current and temperature) and raises a SCADA-style alarm when a reading crosses a safe limit. It shows I can build a circuit, measure it, work safely, and read and draw a single-line diagram.

01 · What I'm building

A bench model of how a feeder is watched.

Out on the network, a remote terminal unit (RTU) in a substation watches a feeder and flags an over-current or over-temperature. I'm building a small, safe version of that on the bench: a microcontroller reads an extra-low-voltage test circuit and trips an alarm past a threshold. It's the trade in miniature: a circuit to build, readings to take, protection logic, a diagram to draw, and a safe way to work on it.

04 · Safety first

How I make it safe before I touch it.

Even on an extra-low-voltage rig, I work the way the trade works. The sequence below follows AS/NZS 4836 and the SafeWork NSW Managing electrical risks in the workplace Code of Practice (WHS Regulation Part 4.7).

  1. Identify & notify. Know what I'm working on and tell anyone nearby.
  2. Shut down & isolate. Operate the energy-isolating device: unplug the plug-pack and open the rig's isolator. A functional switch is not an isolation point.
  3. Lock out & tag out. Personal danger lock plus a danger tag. A tag is not an isolation device; the lock is.
  4. Dissipate stored energy. Discharge any capacitor; treat the battery as a live source and disconnect it.
  5. Prove dead, then test before touch. Prove the tester on a known live source, test the rig dead, then prove the tester live again.
  6. Then work. Only once it's proven dead and locked out.

Built entirely at extra-low voltage, no mains wiring

The whole rig runs from a plug-pack/USB or battery and stays at or below 50 V AC / 120 V DC. In NSW, extra-low voltage is excluded from "electrical installation," so this is not licensable electrical wiring work under the Gas and Electricity (Consumer Safety) Act 2017 and its 2018 Regulation, provided nothing connects to, modifies or hardwires into fixed mains wiring.

Anything touching fixed wiring, switchboards, power points or a hardwired transformer is a licensed electrician's job, and I leave it there. Knowing exactly where that line sits is part of working safely.

05 · The theory behind it

What the build taught me.

Open any topic.

Measurement & Ohm's law numeracy

Voltage, current and resistance tie together as V = I × R, and power as P = V × I. The monitor measures current as the small voltage across a known shunt resistor, then works back to amps. Doing the sums by hand (series and parallel loads, voltage drop) is the same numeracy the aptitude tests check.

Protection & earthing protection

Circuit breakers and fuses interrupt overload and short-circuit current; protection relays detect a fault and trip a breaker; RCDs catch earth leakage. Earthing gives fault current a return path so protection operates inside the required disconnection time (for example 0.4 s on a final subcircuit ≤32 A). The monitor's threshold-and-alarm is the same idea in miniature: sense a fault condition, act on it.

NSW grid structure network

Transgrid runs the high-voltage transmission network (around 500/330/220/132 kV) and feeds the distribution businesses: Ausgrid, Endeavour and Essential. Zone substations step sub-transmission down to about 11–22 kV; distribution substations step that down to 415 V three-phase / 230 V single-phase. Feeders are the 11–33 kV lines between them. Sydney Trains runs its own HV network plus 1500 V DC traction.

Single-line diagrams & wiring colours drawings

An SLD uses one line to stand for a multi-conductor circuit: supply → main switch → protective devices → subcircuits, with the MEN link shown at the main switchboard and every circuit labelled. Symbols follow AS/NZS 60617, kept to one style and never mixed. Under AS/NZS 3000, earth is green/yellow, neutral is blue, and active is brown (among the permitted colours). Reading and drawing these maps straight to pre-apprenticeship unit UEECD0051.

06 · A small bonus

The digital side, briefly.

Because the monitor logs data like a real RTU, it touches the part of the grid that's going digital: SCADA, RTUs and IEC 61850 substation messaging, which Australian utilities secure under the AESCSF.
